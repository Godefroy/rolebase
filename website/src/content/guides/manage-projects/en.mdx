description: 'Run a cross-team project in Rolebase by creating a dedicated role that gathers the people, tasks, discussions, and decisions in one place.'

---

Rolebase does not have a separate "project" object, and it does not need one. A **role** already gathers people, tasks, discussions, decisions, and meetings in a single place, so it makes a natural home for a project. The recommended approach is to create one dedicated role per project, for example a product launch, a client engagement, an event, or a music release.

This guide walks through setting up and running a project this way.

## Why a dedicated role per project

- **Everyone in one place.** The people working on the project sit together in the project role, whatever team they come from.
- **All the work together.** Tasks, discussions, and decisions for the project live in that role instead of being scattered across teams.
- **A clear objective on top.** The role's purpose states what the project is aiming for, so anyone opening it sees the goal right away.
- **A clean history.** When the project ends, you archive the role and keep its full record for reference.

## Before You Begin

Make sure you have:

- An [organization with a few roles](/en/docs/circles-and-roles) already in place.
- The [members](/en/docs/members) who will take part in the project.

## Step 1: Create a role for your project

1. Open the org chart and navigate to the role where the project belongs. A project that spans the whole company can sit under your top-level role, while a team project can sit under that team's role.
2. Click **Add** and select **Role**.
3. Name it after the project, for example "Launch - Spring Album" or "Client - Acme".

<Callout type="tip" title="Group your projects">
  If you run many projects, create a parent "Projects" role and nest each
  project role inside it. Your projects then stay grouped and easy to find in the
  org chart.
</Callout>

## Step 2: Bring in the people involved

A project usually pulls people from several teams. You have two ways to add them to the project role:

- **Add members directly.** Assign the people who are dedicated to the project as members of the role.
- **Invite their roles.** Use [invited roles](/en/docs/circles-and-roles) to bring in a role from another team without moving anyone. A member of the invited role can then take part in the project's work while keeping their home role. This is the cleanest way to run a group that spans production, marketing, legal, and more.

## Step 3: Define the objective

Give the project a direction that everyone can see:

- Use the role's **purpose** to describe what success looks like, for example "Release the spring album on time and reach 50k streams in the first month".
- Add **indicators** to the role to track the metrics that matter, so progress toward the objective stays visible over time.

<Callout type="info" title="A lightweight OKR">
  Rolebase does not have a dedicated OKR feature yet, but a role's purpose paired
  with its indicators gives you a simple way to connect a clear goal to the work
  underneath it.
</Callout>

## Step 4: Break the work into tasks

1. Open the **Tasks** section of the project role.
2. Create the tasks the project needs and assign each one to a member.
3. Set a status and a due date so the team can follow progress.
4. Switch between the **Kanban** and **List** views to plan and track the work the way that suits you.

Because every task belongs to the project role, you get a single board for the whole project, even though the people come from different teams. See the [tasks documentation](/en/docs/tasks) for details.

## Step 5: Coordinate with discussions and decisions

- Open a [discussion](/en/docs/threads) in the project role for anything that needs input, a proposal, or a poll. Everything stays attached to the project rather than lost in a chat tool.
- Record important choices as [decisions](/en/docs/decisions) in the role, so the reasoning behind the project stays documented.

## Step 6: Review progress in meetings

For projects that run over several weeks, schedule a [recurring meeting](/en/docs/meetings) on the project role. A simple agenda works well:

- A **Tasks** step to review what is in progress and what is next.
- A **Discussions** step to go through active discussions and unblock them.
- An **Indicators** step to check progress against the objective.

## Step 7: Close the project

When the project is finished, archive the project role from its actions menu. Everything nested under it, tasks, discussions, decisions, and meetings, is archived together and stays available in the history if you ever need to look back.
