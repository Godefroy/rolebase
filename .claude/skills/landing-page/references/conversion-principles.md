# SaaS Landing Page — Conversion Principles (evidence base)

This is the playbook behind the `landing-page` skill. It separates **verified facts** (with citations) from **best-practice heuristics**. Use the facts to justify priorities; apply the heuristics as craft. Do not cite numbers that are not in the "Verified facts" section — several widely repeated stats were fact-checked and found unsupported (see "Do NOT cite").

---

## How people actually perceive a page (the reason the hero matters)

**Verified facts:**

- First impressions of a website form in **under 0.2 seconds**. Users spend ~2.6s scanning before focusing on a section, then fixate ~180ms per section. (Missouri S&T eye-tracking, 2012; corroborated by Lindgaard et al. 2006, visual appeal judged in ~50ms.)
- In conventional left-aligned (Western) layouts, users spend **~80% of viewing time on the left half** of the page, ~20% on the right. Put the value proposition and primary content upper-left. (NN/g "Horizontal Attention Leans Left", 2010/2017.) Caveat: it is an average and a learned convention; it reverses for right-to-left languages.
- A **dominant headline draws the eye first and gets under one second of attention** — so it must carry a complete, clear value proposition. Clarity beats persuasion. (CXL / NN/g; MECLABS "clarity trumps persuasion" A/B lifts of 38–200%.)
- Users **scan, they do not read** — on average ~28% of page text is read, often in a default F-shaped pattern. (Weinreich et al. 2008 via NN/g.) Caveat: the F-pattern is a *default* for unstructured content, not a law — structure (headings, bold, bullets, layer-cake layout) redirects the eye on purpose.

**Implication:** the above-the-fold hero does most of the work. You have well under a second to communicate relevance. Lead with the outcome, top-left, in a big clear headline.

## Hero / above the fold

The hero must answer four questions, fast and in this order:
1. **What is it?** (category clarity — a visitor must know what kind of thing this is)
2. **Who is it for?** (the visitor self-identifies)
3. **Why is it better / why care?** (the differentiated outcome)
4. **What do I do next?** (one obvious action)

Components of a strong SaaS hero:

- **Headline** — benefit/outcome-led, not feature- or category-led. Describe the transformation the user gets ("Ship support tickets to zero", "Close the books in a day"), not the mechanism ("AI-powered ticketing platform"). Make the first few words count — they get the most attention. One dominant headline, not competing ones.
- **Subheadline** — one or two lines that add the concrete *what/how/who* the headline implies. This is where the plain-language explanation lives.
- **Primary CTA** — one, visually dominant, with action+outcome microcopy ("Start free", "Get a demo"). Not "Submit" / "Learn more".
- **Risk reducer** near the CTA — "No credit card", "Free plan", "Cancel anytime", "2-minute setup".
- **Hero visual** — show the actual product (real UI, a short product video/GIF, or an interactive demo) or the concrete outcome. Real product imagery outperforms abstract stock illustration for comprehension.
- **Early trust signal** — a strip of customer logos, a rating/award badge, or a user count, visible without scrolling or just below the fold.

Headline angles that work for SaaS (pick by audience):
- Outcome/transformation: "[Achieve desired outcome] without [pain]."
- Job-to-be-done: name the job the user hires the product to do.
- Quantified result: "[Metric] up/down by [number]."
- Category + differentiator: when the category is unfamiliar, define it, then differentiate.

Avoid: vague aspirational taglines with no product meaning, internal jargon, feature soup, and clever wordplay that sacrifices clarity.

## Copywriting

**Verified fact (correlational):** landing pages written at a **5th–7th grade reading level convert ~2× higher** (11.1%) than professional-level copy (5.3%). (Unbounce 2024 Conversion Benchmark Report — a vendor benchmark, so an association, not proven causation. But the direction is consistent with all perception research: simpler comprehends faster.)

**Heuristics (craft, not measured here):**

- Write in the **second person** ("you", "your team"). Make the reader the protagonist; the product is the guide, not the hero.
- **Benefit before feature.** State the outcome the user gets, then name the feature that delivers it. A feature is a fact about the product; a benefit is a change in the user's world. (FAB: Feature → Advantage → Benefit.)
- **Concrete over abstract.** Specifics and numbers ("set up in 5 minutes", "used by 400 teams") beat superlatives ("powerful", "the best", "seamless"). Cut adjectives that survive without changing meaning.
- **Short sentences, scannable blocks.** Headings, subheads, bold, and bullets so a scanner gets the gist without reading prose.
- Speak the **customer's language**, mirroring how they describe the problem (voice-of-customer), not internal product vocabulary.

**Message frameworks** (heuristics for structuring persuasive copy — useful scaffolds, no verified lift figures):
- **PAS** — Problem → Agitate → Solution. Name the pain, make it vivid, present the product as relief. Strong for pain-driven purchases.
- **AIDA** — Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. A whole-page arc.
- **FAB** — Feature → Advantage → Benefit. For feature/benefit sections.
- **JTBD** — Jobs To Be Done. Frame the product around the progress the user is trying to make, not demographics.
- **Value proposition** = (what you do) + (for whom) + (the unique benefit / why you over alternatives). It should be expressible in one or two sentences and headline the hero.

## Page structure & section ordering

Order sections to answer the visitor's questions in the sequence they naturally ask. A reliable SaaS blueprint:

1. **Hero** — value proposition + primary CTA (above the fold).
2. **Social proof strip** — logos / rating / count, immediately reinforcing the hero claim.
3. **Problem / stakes** — the pain or cost of the status quo the visitor recognizes (builds relevance and desire).
4. **How it works** — 3-ish steps showing the path to the outcome; reduces "will this work for me?" anxiety.
5. **Benefits / features** — benefit-led blocks, each pairing an outcome with the feature and a visual. Lead with the most differentiated.
6. **Deeper proof** — testimonials with names/titles/photos, a case study or quantified result, more logos.
7. **Objection handling / FAQ** — the real reasons people hesitate, answered plainly.
8. **Final CTA** — restate the value proposition and the primary action; a clean closing ask.

This is a default, not a straitjacket — reorder to fit the audience and offer, but keep the hero's job intact and keep proof close to claims.

**CTA cadence:** repeat the primary action at natural decision points — after the hero, after value is established (post how-it-works/benefits), and at the end. A long page with a single CTA at the top under-converts; one with five competing CTAs confuses. One *primary* action, repeated.

## Social proof & trust

Trust is a precondition for conversion; place proof next to the claims and CTAs it supports.

- **Customer logos** — recognizable names borrow credibility; a strip near the hero is standard.
- **Testimonials** — attributed (name, role, company, photo) and specific (a concrete result or before/after) beat anonymous praise. Place near the relevant benefit or CTA.
- **Quantified outcomes / case studies** — "cut onboarding from 3 weeks to 3 days" is proof; "great product" is not.
- **Ratings & awards** — review-site badges (G2, Capterra), star ratings, "best of" awards.
- **Counts** — users, teams, companies served — signals adoption and reduces perceived risk.
- **Security / compliance** — SOC 2, GDPR, ISO, data residency, SSO — essential for B2B and for any objection about data.

**Honesty rule:** never fabricate proof. If proof is thin, recommend collecting it (ask customers for quotes, add review-site links). A real modest signal beats a fake impressive one, and false claims destroy the trust that converts.

> Note: popular "+34% from testimonials / +270% from reviews / +80% from video" figures did **not** survive fact-checking and are excluded. Use social proof because it builds trust and answers objections, not because of a specific cited percentage.

## CTA design & microcopy

- **One primary action** per page, visually dominant (high-contrast button, consistent color reserved for the primary CTA). Secondary actions are visually subordinate (outline/link), never competing.
- **Microcopy = action + outcome/value**, not generic verbs. "Start free trial", "Get my demo", "Create my org chart" > "Submit", "Click here", "Learn more".
- **Reduce risk at the point of action**: "No credit card", "Free forever plan", "Cancel anytime", "Setup in minutes". The CTA and its risk reducer travel together.
- **Repeat** the primary CTA down the page (see cadence) so the visitor can act the moment they are convinced, without scrolling back.
- Make the button look clickable and large enough to tap on mobile.

## Friction reduction

Every step, field, and decision between intent and action sheds conversions.

**Verified facts (e-commerce, directionally applicable to SaaS signup):**
- The average checkout has **11.3 form fields when ~8 suffice** — roughly a third are removable. (Baymard.)
- **~17–18% of users abandon** specifically because the process is too long/complicated. (Baymard.)

**Apply to SaaS:**
- Ask only for what you truly need *now*. Defer everything else to in-app onboarding. Email + SSO/social login often beats a long form.
- Offer **frictionless entry**: free plan, no credit card, SSO/Google sign-in, instant access or an interactive demo before signup.
- Remove distractions on conversion-focused pages: trim competing nav links and exits that pull away from the primary action.
- Reduce *cognitive* friction too: fewer choices, clearer labels, obvious next step.

> "Cut to 5 fields = +120%" and "11→4 fields = +160%" did **not** survive fact-checking — do not cite them. The directional principle (fewer fields, less abandonment) is sound; the specific multipliers are not.

## Page speed (a direct, measurable conversion lever)

**Verified facts:**
- Every **~100ms of delay can cut conversions ~7%**. (Akamai/SOASTA, Spring 2017, online retail.)
- As load time goes **1s → 10s, mobile bounce probability rises 123%**. (Google/SOASTA, Think with Google, 2017.)
- Optimal load window is **0–4 seconds**; a **1-second load converts ~3× better than a 5-second load** (Portent, ~100M page views). Conversion peaks around 1–2s and drops steeply after 5s.
- Google: **53% of mobile visits are abandoned if a page takes over 3s** to load.

These are correlational and mostly e-commerce, so directional for SaaS — but the direction is unambiguous and large. Treat speed as a first-class conversion feature: optimize images (compress, lazy-load, right-size), defer non-critical JS, ensure the hero renders fast, and measure (Core Web Vitals / Lighthouse). A beautiful page that loads in 6 seconds loses to a plain one that loads in 1.

## Mobile

- A large and growing share of traffic is mobile; the hero, headline, and primary CTA must work on a phone first. The above-the-fold value proposition and CTA should be visible without pinch/zoom.
- Single-column flow, tap-friendly buttons, no horizontal scroll, legible body text without zoom.
- Vertical layouts shift attention toward a top-down (Z/linear) scan — keep the most important message and CTA highest.
- Test the real page on a real viewport; do not assume the desktop layout reflows well.

## Benchmarks (to set expectations, not targets to fake)

**Verified facts (Unbounce 2024, 41K+ pages / 464M pageviews / 57M conversions — medians, correlational vendor data):**
- **All-industry median landing-page conversion: 6.6%** (industry medians range 3.8%–12.3%; e.g. financial services ~8.3%).
- **SaaS median: 3.8%**, with a "good" (75th-percentile) rate of **11.6%**. SaaS converts below the global median — benchmark against the **SaaS band**, not the 6.6% figure.

Use these to calibrate ("a 4% SaaS rate is around median; aim for the double-digit top quartile"), not as guarantees. Real lift comes from the clarity, proof, friction, and speed work above.

## Do NOT cite (failed fact-checking)

These circulate widely but were refuted in adversarial verification — do not present them as fact:
- "Testimonials +34% / displayed reviews +270% / video testimonials +80–86%."
- "5 or fewer form fields = +120%" and "cutting 11→4 fields = +160%."
- "Every 100ms improvement = +1.11% conversions" (the *100ms delay ≈ −7%* figure is supported; this inverse phrasing is not).
- "1-second mobile delay = −20% conversions; conversion peaks at 2.4s / drops below 1% by 4.2s."

When in doubt, prefer a principle stated without a number over a number you cannot source to this file.

## Topics where no verified quantitative evidence was found

The research surfaced no surviving hard numbers for the *lift* of: specific social-proof elements, copywriting frameworks (PAS/AIDA/FAB/JTBD), CTA microcopy variants, FAQ/objection sections, or urgency/scarcity. They remain standard, well-reasoned best practices — apply them as craft and heuristics, and frame them that way. Do not attach invented percentages to them.

### A note on urgency/scarcity

Genuine urgency (a real deadline, limited cohort, expiring price) can nudge action. **Fake urgency erodes trust** and tends to backfire on considered B2B SaaS purchases. Use only if true.
